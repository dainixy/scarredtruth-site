Why I built this
Why I built this

I've been listening to women carry things my whole life.

I was the quiet kid in the corner with two different-colored eyes and a stack of books. My mother's friends would sit in our kitchen with their problems — a job they wanted, a man they were scared of, a life that felt stuck. I listened. Then I'd say what I saw. And somehow, it helped. I've been that person ever since.

Years later, I learned the other side of it. I built a whole life for a woman I loved — the house, the future, all of it — and found out by accident she was building one without me. And before that, I spent years disappearing little by little to keep someone else happy. So when you tell me you don't recognize yourself anymore — I'm not nodding politely. I've been her.

Then, after 35 years of marriage, my father told my mother to leave. She called me. For the first time I heard everything she'd swallowed for years so we wouldn't have to. I quit my job and went to her.

And I watched a woman who had lost her light get it back. Younger. Funnier. Louder when she laughed. She didn't become someone new. She came back to herself.

But she didn't do it alone. Nobody does.

That's why this exists. The first hundred women start on August 1, 2026.

What's inside

Everything the 38 days hold.

  • Every morning: a short note from me. Two minutes, like a voice memo from a friend. If faith is your floor, there's a verse under it — take it or leave it.
  • One small task a day, finishable before lunch. Week one clears the ground. Then the mornings, the mirror, the no — and her own woman.
  • Week four is scripts week: the exact words for when he calls, when family pushes, when someone asks for what you don't have. Practiced once, out loud, before you need them.
  • A private room of women who started the same morning you did — and I introduce you by hand. The woman who wrote almost your exact sentence? She's in here. You'll meet her.
  • I read what you write and answer you by name — every one of the 38 days.
  • Fridays we put the week's small wins in one place. Sundays are rest, on purpose. There is no behind.
  • No video calls, ever. Nothing to install — it opens in your browser, like email. And when it ends, everything stays yours.

The honest questions

"I can't spend money on myself."

I know. You've spent years putting everything into everyone else — that's part of what broke. One woman wrote: "I don't put back into myself what I give to others." So here's the truth about the $27: it's lunch out, once. And it's the first small thing you've put back in a long time. That's not the price of the room. That's day one of the work.

"I wouldn't know what to write."

One line is enough. The day's question does the asking — you just answer it, like a text to a friend. And some women only read for the first week. That's allowed. Nobody performs here.

"Isn't it too late for me?"

My mother rebuilt her whole life after 35 years of marriage. It wasn't too late. It was right on time. There's no age where coming back to yourself stops working — but there's also no reason to give it another year.

"Will the other women judge me?"

Every woman in the room paid to do the same thing you're doing, starting the same morning. Nobody's watching from the bleachers. First names only, the room is locked, and the rules are posted: what's said here stays here — and no advice unless you ask for it.

"Do I have to be on camera? I'm not good with computers."

No cameras, no video calls, no photos — ever. And if you can read an email and click a link, you have every skill this needs. You get one link. You click it. You're in.

"Is this therapy?"

No. I'm not a therapist, and this doesn't replace one. It's a daily practice with people beside you. And if you're in real crisis, you deserve more than any program — in the US, call or text 988, anytime.

"Is this a church thing?"

No. Faith runs quiet through what I write, because it runs through me. But nobody here preaches at you, and you don't have to believe anything to belong.

"What if I fall behind?"

There is no behind. Sundays are rest days on purpose, the tasks don't expire, and nobody keeps score. A missed day is just a missed day. You cannot do this wrong.

"What happens when the 38 days end?"

The daily rhythm ends — and so do my daily replies. That's a promise with an edge on purpose: you won't need me every day forever. You keep everything you wrote and everything I sent. And the room stays open — your seat in it stays yours.

"Why does it cost money when Facebook groups are free?"

Two reasons. Because I answer you every day, myself, for 38 days. And because a room where every woman chose to be there is a different room. Free groups are where you scroll. This is where you show up.
